    ✨ About Ivan Vytivskyi

    I am Ivan Vytivskyi, from a small village in Ukraine called Haivshchyna. I created this app, Ivan’s "The Spelling Wheel", while learning English from scratch.

    Spelling has always been the hardest part of learning English for me. I tried many methods: rewriting the same words over and over in notebooks, hiring teachers (but lessons were too expensive), even making a box of flashcards with misspelled words on one side and the correct spelling on the other. But I noticed a big problem — I was memorizing the wrong spelling first, because I saw it before the correct one.

    I needed a better solution: something that could say the word aloud so I could write it down, and then show the correct spelling afterwards.

    That’s why I built this app — Ivan’s "The Spelling Wheel". It’s simple and easy to use: you spin the wheel, listen to the voice, write the word, and check if you spelled it correctly.

    I hope this tool helps other English learners just like it’s helping me.

    📘 Spelling Tips

    • ✅ Practice often – Short, regular practice works best (10–15 minutes daily).
    • 👂 Listen carefully – Repeat the word after hearing it.
    • ✍ Write it down – Writing helps memory stick.
    • 🔄 Review – Go back to difficult words often.
    • 🎯 Focus – One category at a time is easier.

    📏 Common Spelling Rules

    1. “i before e, except after c”
      Examples: believe, piece, chief, thief.
      After “c”: receive, ceiling, conceive.
      ⚠️ Exceptions you just have to memorize: weird, science, seize, foreign.
    2. Double the final consonant when adding -ing/-ed if the vowel before is short.
      Examples: run → running, big → bigger.
    3. Drop the final “e” when adding a vowel suffix.
      Examples: make → making, hope → hoping.
    4. Change “y” to “i” before adding -es/-ed.
      Examples: baby → babies, carry → carried.
    5. Words ending in “f” often change to -ves in plural.
      Examples: leaf → leaves, wolf → wolves.

    📘 Rules with the Letter "P"

    1. Double “p” after a short vowel
      Examples: hop → hopping, slip → slippery, ship → shipped.
    2. Silent “p” at the start of some words (often from Greek origin).
      Examples: psychology, pneumonia, pterodactyl.
    3. “ph” makes the “f” sound
      Examples: phone, elephant, alphabet.
    4. “pp” vs. “p” in the middle of words – doubling often keeps the vowel short.
      Examples: dinner (short vowel) vs. diner (long vowel); slipper vs. slicer.

    🧠 Memory Tricks

    1. Make up a rhyme or phrase to remember tricky words.
      • "Necessary has one collar and two sleeves" → 1 “c” and 2 “s”.
      • "A piece of pie" → reminds you that piece has "ie".
      • "Rhythm Helps Your Two Hips Move" → to spell rhythm.
    2. Break long words into smaller parts.
      • definitely → de-fi-nite-ly
      • environment → en-vi-ron-ment
    3. Link the spelling to meaning.
      Example: "Desert" (arid place) has one “s” because it is empty; "Dessert" (sweet treat) has two “s” because you always want more.
    4. Create a picture in your mind that connects the word to its meaning.
      Example: imagine a "school" with two "o"s as the wheels of a bus.
